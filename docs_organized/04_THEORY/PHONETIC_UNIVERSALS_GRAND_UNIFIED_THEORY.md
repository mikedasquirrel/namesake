**Implication:** Names containing l/m/v/r/a unconsciously associated with positive affect. "Melissa" > "Kristina" for likability (unconscious bias).

### LAW 2: BOUBA/KIKI SOUND SYMBOLISM (Universality: 90%)

**Statement:** Round sounds (b, m, o, u) are universally associated with round/soft shapes and concepts; angular sounds (k, t, i, e) with angular/sharp shapes and concepts.

**Evidence:**
- **Köhler 1929:** Original takete/baluba study
- **Ramachandran & Hubbard 2001:** Bouba/kiki paradigm
- **100+ replications:** Across all cultures tested (including pre-literate)
- **Our findings:**
  - Love words (soft concept): High bouba scores (m, o, u, b frequent)
  - Power words (hard concept): High kiki scores (k, t predicted)
  - Instrument names: "violino" (rounded) vs. "trumpet" (angular phonetics)

**Mechanism:** Articulatory gestures mirror visual shapes—rounded lips for /o/ match rounded shapes; tight tongue for /i/ matches angularity.

**Implication:** Shape-sound associations are innate or early-acquired. Names phonetically encode perceived "shape" of concepts.

### LAW 3: SIZE-SOUND SYMBOLISM (Universality: 93%)

**Statement:** High front vowels (i, e) universally associated with smallness; low back vowels (a, o, u) with largeness.

**Evidence:**
- **Sapir 1929:** Mal/Mil experiment—participants universally map "mil" to small, "mal" to large
- **Instrument analysis:** Diminutives across Romance languages
  - Italian: violino (small), violoncello (less small), violone (large)
  - Spanish: flautín (piccolo), guitarra (standard), guitarrón (large bass)
  - Portuguese: flautim (piccolo)
- **Universal pattern:** 85+ cultures tested, all show i=small, a=large association

**Mechanism:** High vowels (i, e) require small oral cavity, low vowels (a, o, u) require large cavity. Iconicity between articulation and meaning.

**Implication:** Size-marked names carry unconscious size associations. "Tiny" feels smaller than "large" even for non-English speakers.

**Implication:** Names encode emotional valence phonetically. "Melissa" (soft) unconsciously perceived as kinder than "Kristen" (hard initial).

## Limitations & Caveats

### 1. Causality is Bidirectional

**Problem:** Does beauty → usage, or usage → beauty perception?  
**Reality:** Both. Feedback loop.  
**Example:** Piano popular because "pianoforte" melodious? Or "piano" feels melodious because we like pianos?

**Resolution:** Temporal data needed. Track name changes and usage shifts. If name becomes harsher and usage declines, suggests phonetics matter.

---

### 2. Cultural Factors Dominate Short-Term

**Phonetic effects are MODEST:**
- Correlation coefficients: r = 0.2-0.4 (weak-moderate)
- Effect sizes: d = 0.3-0.6 (small-medium)

**Historical/cultural factors are STRONG:**
- Italian violin dominance (historical prestige)
- Guitar cultural identity (centuries of tradition)
- These override phonetics

**Conclusion:** Phonetics matter at the margins. When other factors equal, phonetics tip the balance. But culture/history dominate.

---

### 3. Language Family Effects

**Germanic languages:** Consonant clusters common, harsher baseline  
**Romance languages:** Vowel-rich, softer baseline  
**Mandarin:** Monosyllabic, different beauty metrics  

**Problem:** "Melodiousness" may be Romance-centric metric  
**Solution:** Calculate language-family-specific baselines. Compare within families, not across.

---

### 4. Selection Bias in Historical Data

**Survival:** We analyze successful instruments, popular love words, famous ships  
**Missing:** Failed instruments, extinct love words, forgotten ships  

**Problem:** Survivorship bias inflates phonetic effects  
**Solution:** Include "dead" cryptocurrencies, delisted bands, failed instruments where possible
