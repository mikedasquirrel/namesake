## The Quantum Measurement Problem As A Naming Problem

The measurement problem in quantum mechanics is that observation collapses the wave function. Before measurement, particles exist in superposition - all possibilities at once. After measurement, one outcome becomes real. But what is measurement? It's not just looking. It's recording. It's creating information. It's making a mark that says "this happened."

Measurement is naming. You're taking an indefinite possibility space and collapsing it to a definite outcome by giving it a label. "The electron is here." "The spin is up." The act of naming - of putting a linguistic handle on the observation - is what collapses the wave function.

This sounds like metaphysics but it might be physics. Information theory and quantum mechanics are deeply connected. Information has to be stored in physical systems. Creating information changes entropy. The act of recording an outcome - giving it a name - physically changes the system.

## The Double Slit Experiment: Naming Determines Reality

In the double slit experiment, photons act like waves when you're not measuring which slit they go through. They act like particles when you are measuring. The famous interpretation is that observation collapses the wave function.

But "observation" is too vague. What collapses it? Some interpretations say it's the physical interaction. Others say it's consciousness. But maybe it's simpler: it's naming. When you set up a detector that will tell you "the photon went through slit A," you've created a system that forces nature to give a nameable answer. That's what collapses the wave.

Without the naming apparatus - without a way to say "A" or "B" - the photon doesn't have to choose. It can be both. It's only when you force nature to give you a nameable answer that nature picks one.

This suggests something wild: reality at the quantum level is fundamentally indefinite until naming occurs. Not until measurement, but until the measurement can be translated into linguistic categories. The electron isn't "here" or "there" until we have those categories and force nature to put it in one.

If this is true, then naming isn't secondary to reality - it's constitutive of it. At least at the quantum level, things don't fully exist until they can be named.
