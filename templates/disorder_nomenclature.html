Psychiatric Nomenclature Research

how disorder names influence clinical outcomes and patient survival

research overview

This research program investigates whether psychiatric disorder names influence clinical outcomes through stigma-mediated pathways. Analysis of 50 disorders indicates name phonetic harshness correlates with elevated stigma and reduced treatment seeking. Historical evidence from major nomenclature changes suggests medical, neutral naming may facilitate improved outcomes.

As DSM-6 development proceeds, evidence-based naming principles may optimize outcomes for emerging technology-related conditions. We propose specific nomenclature for AI-era disorders validated through survey experiments.

Cross-sectional analysis demonstrates disorder name phonetic harshness correlates strongly with stigma scores (r = 0.68, p < .001, n = 47). Path analysis indicates stigma mediates approximately 87% of the relationship between name harshness and treatment-seeking rates. Historical name changes associate with substantial outcome improvements, though causal attribution remains limited by observational designs.

historical name changes

post-traumatic stress disorder

The transition from "Shell Shock" to "PTSD" (1980) preceded increased treatment rates from 8% to 58% and research funding increases of 4,700%. While confounded by advocacy and policy changes, temporal associations suggest nomenclature contributed.

medical framing

intellectual disability

Replacement of "Mental Retardation" (2010) correlated with 60% stigma reduction, 22 percentage point employment gains, and 35 point inclusion increases. Outcome acceleration post-2010 exceeded pre-existing trends.

stigma reduction

bipolar disorder

Transition from "Manic-Depressive Illness" (1980) associated with 25-30% stigma reduction and improved public understanding. Neutral medical framing appeared to reduce fear-based perceptions.

neutral framing

evidence base

Path analysis indicates stigma mediates 87% of this relationship, suggesting nomenclature influences outcomes primarily through public perception rather than direct clinical effects.

Analysis combines cross-sectional correlation studies, interrupted time series of historical name changes, and experimental validation through survey designs. While observational limitations preclude definitive causal claims, converging evidence across multiple methodologies strengthens inference.

Optimal phonetic profile: harshness 45-55, syllables 3-6, pronounceable acronyms, medical/Latin roots, neutral descriptors. These principles balance clinical authority with accessibility, potentially facilitating treatment access while maintaining diagnostic legitimacy.
