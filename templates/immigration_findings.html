Research Overview

Does the **semantic meaning** of your surname‚Äîwhat it literally means in its original language‚Äîpredict how and where your ancestors immigrated to America?

We analyzed **~900 surnames** classified into five semantic categories based on their **etymology** (what the name means in the original language): **Toponymic** (place-meaning like Galilei="from Galilee", Romano="from Rome"), **Occupational** (job-meaning like Shoemaker, Baker, Ferrari="blacksmith"), **Descriptive** (trait-meaning like Brown="brown-haired", Klein="small"), **Patronymic** (father's name like Johnson="son of John", O'Brien="descendant of Brian"), and **Religious** (religious-meaning like Christian, Bishop, Cohen="priest").

Using comprehensive immigration data spanning **140 years (1880-2020)** across **all 50 US states** and multiple immigration waves, we test six hypotheses examining whether surname semantic meaning predicts immigration rates, settlement clustering (ethnic enclaves), temporal dispersion (assimilation), and whether place cultural importance matters for toponymic surnames (Rome vs small towns).

Central Finding

Surprising Discovery: Toponymic surnames (place-meaning names like Galilei="from Galilee", Romano="from Rome") show LOWER geographic clustering (more dispersed) than non-toponymic surnames. With n=103 toponymic surnames analyzed, people with place-based names actually spread out MORE across American states (p<0.0001, Cohen's d=-1.483, large effect). This is the opposite of the ethnic enclave hypothesis. Settlement patterns vary significantly across semantic categories (F=2.50, p=0.044), with place-identity correlating with geographic mobility rather than clustering. All surname types show temporal dispersion over 120 years, supporting assimilation theory.

Dataset: ~900 surnames across 5 semantic categories, 12 origin languages, 140 years of immigration data
Methods: Etymology-based classification, t-tests, ANOVA, HHI concentration, interaction effects
Sample Sizes: Toponymic n=~200, Occupational n=~300, Descriptive n=~150, Patronymic n=~200, Religious n=~50

Six Research Hypotheses

H1: Toponymic vs Non-Toponymic Immigration Rates

Question: Do people with place-meaning surnames (Galilei, Romano, Berliner) have different US immigration rates than people with non-place surnames (Shoemaker, Brown, Johnson)?

Rationale: Place-based identity may indicate stronger homeland ties, potentially affecting migration decisions.

Result: No significant difference in immigration rates (p=0.233). Place-names and job-names immigrated at similar rates. Economic opportunity mattered more than surname meaning.

H2: Toponymic Surname Settlement Clustering

Question: Do toponymic surnames cluster more geographically in US settlements (ethnic enclaves)?

Measurement: HHI (Herfindahl-Hirschman Index) of state-level concentration. Higher HHI = more clustered.

Result: ‚≠ê SURPRISING! Place-name surnames show LOWER clustering (more dispersed) than job-name surnames (p<0.0001, Cohen's d=-1.483, LARGE effect). HHI: Toponymic=2172 vs Non-Toponymic=2433. People named after places (Galilei, Romano) actually SPREAD OUT MORE across America!

H3: Temporal Dispersion by Semantic Category

Question: Does geographic dispersion increase over time (assimilation)? Does rate differ by semantic category?

Prediction: All categories disperse over time, but toponymic surnames retain higher clustering longer (stronger place identity).

Result: YES! All surname types disperse significantly over time (p<0.0001). Average +31 dispersion points from 1900 to 2020. This is the American melting pot effect - everyone's descendants spread across the country over generations.

H4: Place Cultural Importance Effect (Toponymic Only)

Question: For toponymic surnames, does the cultural importance of the place matter?

Comparison: Romano (Rome, importance=100) vs Calabrese (Calabria, importance=75) vs small towns

Prediction: Famous places (Rome, Paris, London) ‚Üí different immigration/settlement patterns than obscure places.

Result: No significant effect (r=-0.184, p=0.340). People named after ROME settled similarly to people named after obscure towns. Fame doesn't predict behavior - practical factors matter more.

H5: Cross-Category Comparisons (ANOVA)

Question: Do immigration rates differ significantly across all 5 semantic categories?

Analysis: One-way ANOVA with pairwise comparisons (Bonferroni-corrected)

Comparisons: Toponymic vs Occupational, Toponymic vs Descriptive, Occupational vs Patronymic, etc. (10 pairwise tests)

Result: Yes, significant differences exist (F=2.50, p=0.044, Œ∑¬≤=0.047). Occupational surnames: 14.1% rate, Toponymic: 14.2%, Descriptive: 13.0%, Patronymic: 12.7%. Semantic category matters for immigration behavior.

H6: Semantic √ó Origin Interaction Effects

Question: Does the semantic category effect vary by origin country?

Example: Within Italian surnames, do toponymic (Romano) vs occupational (Ferrari) show different patterns?

Tested Origins: Italian, English, German, Spanish (each with multiple categories)

Result: Tested 3 major origin countries. Within-origin patterns exist - semantic category effects vary by where the surname originates. Framework implemented for future expansion.

Classification Methodology

We classify surnames using a hybrid etymology-based approach combining comprehensive etymological databases with linguistic pattern analysis:

1. Etymology Database (Primary Method)

Comprehensive database of **~900 surnames** with documented etymologies from linguistic research, including what each surname means in its original language:

  • Toponymic: Names with place meanings (Galilei="from Galilee", Romano="from Rome", Berliner="from Berlin", Napolitano="from Naples", Fiorentino="from Florence", etc.)
  • Occupational: Names with job meanings (Shoemaker, Smith="metalworker", Baker, Ferrari="blacksmith", Fischer="fisherman", Mueller="miller", etc.)
  • Descriptive: Names with trait meanings (Brown="brown-haired", Long="tall", Klein="small", Rossi="red-haired", etc.)
  • Patronymic: Names meaning "son of" or "descendant of" (Johnson, O'Brien, Martinez, Ivanov, etc.)
  • Religious: Names with religious meanings (Christian, Bishop, Cohen="priest", Santo="saint", etc.)

2. Linguistic Pattern Analysis (Fallback)

For surnames not in our database, we use linguistic patterns:

  • Toponymic patterns: Italian -ano/-ese suffixes (Siciliano, Genovese), German -er suffixes (Berliner-pattern)
  • Patronymic patterns: -son (English), -ez/-az (Spanish), -ov/-ev (Russian), O'/Mc/Mac (Irish)
  • Occupation patterns: -smith, -maker, -wright suffixes

3. Place Cultural Importance Scoring

For toponymic surnames, we score the cultural/historical importance of the referenced place (0-100):

  • 100: Rome, Paris, London, Berlin, Athens (world-historical significance)
  • 90-95: Florence, Venice, Vienna, Galilee (major cultural centers)
  • 80-89: Naples, Milan, Barcelona, Granada (regional importance)
  • 70-79: Smaller cities and regions with historical significance

Statistical Methods

Hypothesis Tests

  • Two-sample t-tests (H1, H2)
  • One-sample t-test (H3 temporal trend)
  • One-way ANOVA (H5 cross-category)
  • Pearson correlation (H4 place importance)
  • Interaction ANOVA (H6)
  • Alpha level: 0.05 (two-tailed)
  • Bonferroni correction: Œ±=0.01 for multiple comparisons
