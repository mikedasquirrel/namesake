Why This Finding Is Critical

We predicted this domain would show the strongest nominative effects (r = 0.35-0.40) because performers consciously choose stage names for career optimization. Instead, it shows the weakest effects (r = 0.00). This reversal is more valuable than confirmation.

What we learned: When performance is visually accessible and immediately evaluable, names become irrelevant. The content speaks for itself. Names only matter when they're the primary filter - when you can't see/judge the performance before committing.

The Visibility Hypothesis

Audio/Invisible Performance: Names matter (Bands r=0.19, predicted Podcasts r=0.15-0.25)

Mixed Media: Names matter moderately (Films r=0.14)

Visual/Visible Performance: Names don't matter (Adult Film r=0.00)

This is not a failure of nominative determinism - this is discovery of its boundary condition. We now know WHERE it works and where it doesn't.

Language & Authenticity Patterns

Language distribution (sample of 100):

  • Anglo names: 96% (overwhelming majority)
  • Asian names: 4% (Asa Akira, Marica Hase, etc.)
  • Latino names: <1% (Valentina Nappi, etc.)

Key insight: Despite visual medium where ethnicity is immediately visible, performers overwhelmingly choose Anglo-sounding stage names. This suggests:

  • Anglicization for broader market appeal
  • Stage names as fantasy/brand (not authentic identity)
  • Ethnic markers potentially limiting when appearance already signals ethnicity

Hypothesis for non-visual contrast: In audio media (podcasts), authentic ethnic names may perform better because you can't see ethnicity - the name BECOMES the identity signal rather than conflicting with visual presentation.
