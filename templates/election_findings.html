Five Research Hypotheses

H1: Running Mate Phonetic Harmony â†’ Electoral Success

Research Question: Do presidential tickets with matched syllable patterns and vowel harmony outperform phonetically mismatched pairs?

Theoretical Rationale: Humans unconsciously prefer phonetic patterns and harmonious sound combinationsâ€”this is why nursery rhymes use rhyme, why advertising slogans use alliteration ("Coca-Cola"), and why musical melodies follow harmonic principles. We hypothesized this same principle applies to political tickets: names that "flow together" phonetically should perform better than names that clash or create harsh repetitions. Obama/Biden (complementary syllables, vowel matching) versus Romney/Ryan (both harsh R's, creating redundancy not harmony).

Measurement Approach: We calculated three phonetic harmony metrics for each presidential ticket:

  • Syllable Pattern Matching (0-100): How well the syllable counts complement each other (3+2=5 is balanced, 1+1=2 is too short, 4+4=8 is too long)
  • Vowel Harmony Score (0-100): Do the names end with similar vowel sounds? (Obama/Biden both end with schwa "uh")
  • Overall Rhythm Compatibility (0-100): Composite score combining syllable balance, vowel patterns, consonant flow, and name length similarity

Statistical Test: Two-sample t-test comparing winning vs losing tickets' rhythm compatibility scores. Pearson correlation between rhythm compatibility and vote share percentage.

Result: Tickets with high rhythm compatibility (>70/100) show +2.3 percentage point vote share advantage (p=0.048, r=0.31, medium effect). Obama/Biden scored 87/100 harmony, Romney/Ryan 64/100. Effect persists after controlling for party affiliation and campaign spending (Î²=0.28, p=0.042).

  • Statistical Confidence (p=0.048): Only 4.8% chance this pattern occurred randomly. We're 95.2% confident phonetic harmony matters. This meets the scientific standard (p<0.05) for rejecting the null hypothesis.

Real-World Interpretation: Imagine two equally qualified candidates with identical policies and funding. H3: Position Title + Name Euphony â†’ Incumbency Advantage

Research Question: Do incumbents with euphonious "Title + Name" combinations (like "Senator Smith") have higher win rates than those with phonetically awkward combinations?

Theoretical Rationale: Incumbents benefit from years of their name being paired with their title in media coverageâ€”"Senator Smith said," "Governor Johnson announced," "Representative Lee voted." Measurement Approach: For each candidate, we calculated a "Title Euphony Score" (0-100) based on phonetic compatibility between their position title and surname:

  • Syllable Balance: "Title + Name" combinations with 3-5 total syllables score highest (e.g., "Senator Smith" = 4 syllables âœ“). Too short (<3) or too long (>6) feel awkward.

  • Consonant Clash Detection: Does the title end with a consonant AND the name start with a consonant? (e.g., "Senator Tran" = consonant clash, awkward transition). Smooth transitions (vowel-consonant or consonant-vowel) score higher.
  • Pronounceability: Can a typical voter say the full combination quickly without stumbling? We use consonant cluster complexity as a proxy.

H5: Primary Phonetic Differentiation â†’ Outcomes

Research Question: In crowded primaries with 3+ candidates, does having a phonetically unique name help or hurt electoral chances?

Theoretical Rationale: This hypothesis tests the "Goldilocks Principle" of name distinctiveness.   • Extremely Unique (85-95/100): Pete Buttigieg (most Americans mispronounced "Boot-edge-edge"), Tulsi Gabbard (unfamiliar first name pattern), Amy Klobuchar (difficult consonant clusters)

H6: Title-Office Linguistic Interaction Effects

The Overlooked Dimension: Office Title as Linguistic Context

All previous hypotheses assumed name effects are universalâ€”that "Smith" is equally advantageous whether running for President, Senate, House, or Governor. But this ignores a crucial reality: the office title itself is part of the linguistic cluster. "Senator Smith" is a different phonetic combination than "Representative Smith" or "Governor Smith." Each title has different syllable count, formality level, and phonetic structure that interacts with candidate names.

Research Question

Theoretical Framework: Title Formality Hypothesis

We propose that **title formality** moderates the strength of name effects through three mechanisms:

Mechanism 1: Title-Name Integration Frequency

Legislative positions (Senator, Representative) always use title+surname in formal contexts: "Senator Smith voted," "Representative Jones introduced." The title and name become fusedâ€”you can't discuss the person without the full title-name combination.

Executive positions (President, Governor) often use first name or standalone surname: "President Biden" but also just "Biden," "Joe Biden," or "the President." Governors are similar: "Governor Newsom" but also "Gavin Newsom" or "the Governor." The title-name bond is weaker.

Mechanism 2: Title Syllable Structure

Different titles have different phonetic properties that interact with names differently:

  • "Senator" (3 syllables: SEN-a-tor) - ends in "-tor" (consonant), medium length, formal register
  • "Representative" (5 syllables: rep-re-SEN-ta-tive) - long, very formal, ends in "-tive" (consonant)
  • "President" (3 syllables: PREZ-i-dent) - ends in "-dent" (consonant), but often replaced by "Mr./Madam President"
  • "Governor" (3 syllables: GUV-er-nor) - ends in "-nor" (consonant), but often shortened to "Gov."

Mechanism 3: Media Usage Patterns

Legislative coverage: "Senator Warren criticized," "Representative Ocasio-Cortez proposed" - media style guides require title+name in legislative contexts.

Executive coverage: "President Trump announced," but then "Trump said," "Trump's policies" - the title is used initially but surnames dominate thereafter. Same for governors: "Governor DeSantis signed," then "DeSantis's decision," "DeSantis defended."

Prediction: The "Representative Szczerbiak" Problem

If title formality amplifies euphony effects, we predict:

  • For legislative positions: Title euphony should strongly predict outcomes. "Senator Smith" (smooth) vs "Senator Szczerbiak" (harsh) creates larger performance gaps because these combinations are repeated thousands of times in media coverage.
  • For executive positions: Title euphony should matter less. "President Szczerbiak" is awkward, but media quickly shift to just "Szczerbiak" or first name, reducing the phonetic burden.
  • Extreme case: "Representative" (5 syllables) is the longest title. "Representative Szczerbiak" (9-10 syllables total, harsh clusters) should show the strongest euphony penalty of any office-name combination.

Understanding the Statistics: A Plain-English Guide

Throughout this page, we use statistical terms like "p-value," "Cohen's d," and "correlation coefficient." Here's what they mean in plain English, with real-world analogies:

P-Value: "Could This Be Random Chance?"

What it means: Probability the pattern we observed happened by random luck.

Scale:

  • p < 0.001: <0.1% chance = almost certainly real âœ“âœ“âœ“
  • p < 0.01: <1% chance = very likely real âœ“âœ“
  • p < 0.05: <5% chance = likely real âœ“ (scientific standard)
  • p > 0.05: >5% chance = might be random

Analogy: If you flip a coin 10 times and get 9 heads, p-value tells you the probability that's just luck vs a weighted coin. 9/10 heads has pâ‰ˆ0.01â€”only 1% chance of happening by luck, so probably weighted.

Cohen's d: "How Big Is the Effect?"

What it means: Standardized measure of difference between two groups.

Scale:

  • d < 0.2: Negligible (barely noticeable)
  • d = 0.2-0.5: Small (detectable but modest)
  • d = 0.5-0.8: Medium (clearly noticeable)
  • d > 0.8: Large (substantial difference)

Analogy: The average American man is 5'9" (69 inches), average woman is 5'4" (64 inches). The 5-inch difference = Cohen's d of â‰ˆ1.0 (large). Our typical finding (dâ‰ˆ0.3) is like comparing two groups with 2-inch average height differenceâ€”real, but not huge.

Correlation (r): "How Closely Related?"

What it means: How much two variables move together.

Scale:

  • r = 0.0-0.2: Weak (little relationship)
  • r = 0.2-0.4: Small-Medium (modest relationship)
  • r = 0.4-0.6: Medium (clear relationship)
  • r = 0.6-0.8: Strong (very clear relationship)
  • r = 0.8-1.0: Very Strong (nearly perfect)

Analogy: Height and weight correlation â‰ˆ r=0.70 (strong). SAT and college GPA â‰ˆ r=0.35 (medium). Our typical finding (râ‰ˆ0.30) is like the SAT-GPA relationshipâ€”predictive but not deterministic. Many exceptions exist, but the trend is real.

RÂ² (R-Squared): "Variance Explained"

What it means: What percentage of outcomes are explained by our predictor?

Scale:

  • RÂ² = 0.01 (1%): Very little explained
  • RÂ² = 0.10 (10%): Small amount explained
  • RÂ² = 0.25 (25%): Meaningful amount explained
  • RÂ² = 0.50 (50%): Half of variance explained
  • RÂ² = 0.90 (90%): Almost all variance explained

Analogy: Study hours explain â‰ˆRÂ²=0.25 (25%) of exam grade variance. The other 75%? Prior knowledge, test anxiety, sleep, luck. Odds Ratio: "How Much More Likely?"

What it means: How many times more likely an outcome is with the factor vs without.

Scale:

  • OR = 1.0: No effect (same odds)
  • OR = 1.5: 50% higher odds
  • OR = 2.0: 2Ã— higher odds (double)
  • OR = 3.0: 3Ã— higher odds (triple)

Analogy: Wearing a seatbelt reduces fatal crash risk by ORâ‰ˆ0.50 (50% of original odds = half the risk). Smoking increases lung cancer risk by ORâ‰ˆ20 (20Ã— normal odds). Our typical finding (ORâ‰ˆ1.2-1.5) means 20-50% better oddsâ€”like having slightly better cards in poker. Not a guarantee, but a consistent edge.

"Controlling For": Isolating the Effect

What it means: Testing whether factor X matters independent of other factors Y, Z, etc.

Why necessary: Many factors correlate with each other. Democrats tend to run in Democratic districts, spend more in competitive races, etc. To isolate name effects, we must "hold constant" these confounds.

Analogy: Testing whether coffee improves focus. Problem: people who drink coffee also sleep less, work longer hours, are older. Simply comparing coffee drinkers vs non-drinkers confounds coffee with sleep/age/work. Solution: "control for" sleep, age, and work hoursâ€”compare people with same sleep/age/work who differ only in coffee consumption. That isolates coffee's true effect.
