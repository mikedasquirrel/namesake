Causal Considerations & Limitations

Correlation does not equal causation. NBA findings require careful interpretation given confounding variables.

Confound 1: Selection Bias

Scouts may unconsciously favor certain name profiles ‚Üí confounds position assignment and draft position. "Strong-sounding" name ‚Üí scouted as center ‚Üí assigned center position. Name didn't cause position; scouts' stereotypes caused both name attention and position assignment.

Confound 2: Media Coverage Bias

Journalists favor easy-to-spell names ‚Üí more hype ‚Üí higher draft position ‚Üí more opportunities ‚Üí career success. Memorability correlates with success via media pathway, not direct nominative effect.

Confound 3: Cultural Background

International players bring different training systems, playing styles, and cultural approaches to basketball. Name complexity correlates with international origin, which correlates with European-style play. Separating name effects from cultural training effects requires sophisticated causal inference.

Critical Caveat: Correlation ‚â† Causation

All NBA findings are correlational, not causal. Position prediction (68% accuracy) proves phonetics contain genuine information, but whether names cause position assignments vs. merely correlate through scouting biases remains unresolved. Experimental designs (randomly assign positions, track outcomes) would be required for causal claims‚Äîobviously infeasible in professional sports.
